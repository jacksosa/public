# PART 1 — UNDERSTAND THIS MEETING


The meeting has been described to you as a "catch-up" requested by HR / school
leadership to understand your recovery and discuss possible next steps. No
indication has been given that this is formal.

HOWEVER: It is important to understand that meetings with HR can take several
forms, and the label "catch-up" is not legally defined. Below are the possible
meeting types this could be, what to expect from each, and how to tell which
you are in.


1.1 INFORMAL WELFARE MEETING / CATCH-UP

Purpose: Genuinely to check on your wellbeing, see how you are, and understand
         whether you feel ready to think about returning. No decisions are
         made. No formal notes go on your file (though a brief record may be
         kept).
Who attends: Typically your line manager only. HR presence is less common but
             possible.
Normal questions: How are you feeling? How is your recovery going? Is there
                  anything we can do to support you?
Documents: Usually none.
Supportive indicators: Relaxed tone, questions about you personally, no
                       reference to policies or procedures, no note-taking.
Common outcome: An agreement to stay in touch. Possibly a date for a follow-up
                conversation. No pressure.


1.2 RETURN-TO-WORK DISCUSSION

Purpose: A structured conversation about your readiness to return and what
         support you might need. This sits between informal and formal.
Who attends: Line manager and possibly HR.
Normal questions: What does your GP/consultant say? Have you thought about
                  what a return might look like? Do you feel ready to discuss
                  adjustments?
Documents: May reference a fit note or consultant letter if you have one.
Supportive indicators: Questions are forward-looking and practical. They ask
                       what you want. They mention phased return or
                       adjustments voluntarily.
Common outcome: A provisional plan. An agreement to involve Occupational Health.
                No binding decisions.


1.3 OCCUPATIONAL HEALTH REFERRAL DISCUSSION

Purpose: The employer wants to refer you to Occupational Health (OH) for an
         independent assessment of your fitness for work and any adjustments
         needed.
Who attends: HR and/or line manager. OH is separate — you would attend that
             appointment later.
Normal questions: Would you consent to an OH referral? Do you have any
                  questions about the process?
Documents: OH referral form. You should be shown what is being sent.
Formal indicators: Consent form may be presented. Clear mention of OH.
Common outcome: OH appointment arranged. OH report to follow (you see it before
                the employer does).

Source: ACAS — Returning to work after absence (acas.org.uk, 2024)


1.4 ABSENCE MANAGEMENT REVIEW

Purpose: The employer is reviewing your absence under their sickness absence
         policy. This is more formal.
Who attends: HR and line manager.
Normal questions: What is your current medical status? When might you be able
                  to return? What support do you need?
Documents: Absence record, any fit notes, possibly sickness absence policy.
Formal indicators: Policy is mentioned. Notes are taken. Reference to "stages"
                   or "triggers." A follow-up meeting date is set.
Common outcome: A formal plan. Possible referral to OH. If return is unlikely
                in the short term, discussion of next steps under the policy.


1.5 FORMAL SICKNESS REVIEW / CAPABILITY PROCESS

Purpose: A formal stage of the employer's capability procedure, which may
         ultimately lead to dismissal on grounds of capability (health). This
         is the most serious type of meeting.
Who attends: HR and a senior manager. You may be entitled to bring a companion
             (trade union rep or work colleague).
Normal questions: Detailed medical questions. What is your prognosis? Can you
                  perform your role with or without adjustments? When do you
                  expect to return?
Documents: Formal invitation letter stating this is a capability hearing.
           Medical evidence. Sickness absence policy. Notes taken.
Formal indicators: Letter states "formal," "capability," or "hearing." Right
                   to be accompanied is mentioned. Possible outcomes (including
                   dismissal) are referenced.
Common outcome: A decision: return with adjustments, extended absence with
                review date, ill-health retirement, or dismissal on capability
                grounds. You have appeal rights.

Source: GOV.UK — Dismissal: your rights (gov.uk, 2026); ACAS — Managing
        absence and returning to work (acas.org.uk, 2024)

  - "Could you let me know what the purpose of this meeting is — is it an

# PART 2 — MEDICAL EXPLANATION (PLAIN ENGLISH)


This section explains, in plain English, the medical context relevant to the
meeting. It is based on established medical knowledge about the conditions and
procedures described. It does not assess your individual capability.


2.1 WHAT IS BOERHAAVE SYNDROME?

Boerhaave syndrome is a spontaneous rupture (tear) of the oesophagus (the tube
connecting your mouth to your stomach). It is a rare and life-threatening
condition. It typically occurs after forceful vomiting or retching, but can
also occur after severe coughing — as in your case.

The rupture allows stomach contents, air, and bacteria to leak into the chest
cavity, causing severe infection and inflammation. Without emergency surgery,
it is almost always fatal.

Source: Surgical literature (Royal College of Surgeons — general surgical
        knowledge base). Confidence: HIGH.


2.2 THE SURGERY: "BOERHAAVE'S LAPAROTOMY REPAIR OVER T TUBE"

A laparotomy is open abdominal surgery. In this procedure:

  - The surgeon opens the abdomen to access the oesophagus.
  - The tear in the oesophagus is repaired (sewn closed).
  - A T-tube is placed to allow drainage and to protect the repair site while
    it heals. The T-tube is a small drainage tube shaped like a T that sits in
    the oesophagus.

This is major surgery. It involves general anaesthesia, a significant incision,
and a prolonged period of healing.

Source: Surgical textbooks / RCSEng general surgical knowledge. Confidence: HIGH.


2.3 RECOVERY AFTER MAJOR OESOPHAGEAL SURGERY

Recovery from this type of surgery is typically measured in months, not weeks.
Key phases:

  - Hospital stay: Several weeks to months (your case: approximately 3 months).
  - Intensive Care: A period in ICU is common after this surgery due to the
    risk of infection, respiratory complications, and the need for close
    monitoring. (Your case: approximately 1 month in ICU.)
  - Nutrition recovery: Eating is often not possible immediately after surgery.
    Nutrition is provided via a feeding tube. Gradual reintroduction of oral
    intake occurs over weeks. (Your case: feeding tube removed in January.)
  - Physical recovery: Fatigue is normal and can persist for many months.
    Muscle loss from prolonged bed rest is expected. Stamina returns gradually.

Source: NHS guidance on major surgery recovery. Confidence: HIGH.

UNKNOWN (individual): The exact rate of recovery. The current state of
healing. Current physical capability.


2.4 RECOVERY AFTER PROLONGED HOSPITALISATION AND ICU

A stay in intensive care and months in hospital has well-documented effects on
the body, regardless of the original condition:

ESTABLISHED EVIDENCE:

  - Muscle wasting (loss of strength): Prolonged bed rest causes significant
    loss of muscle mass. Recovery takes months of gradual activity.
  - Fatigue: Post-ICU fatigue syndrome is recognised in medical literature.
    Patients often report fatigue lasting 6-12 months or longer after
    discharge.
  - Concentration and cognitive effects: Many patients experience "brain fog,"
    difficulty concentrating, or memory issues after prolonged ICU stays. This
    is temporary for most people but can last months.
  - Sleep disturbance: Disrupted sleep patterns are common after hospitalisation.
  - Emotional adjustment: Anxiety, low mood, or loss of confidence are common
    and normal after a life-threatening illness.

Source: NHS — Recovery after intensive care; NICE guidance on rehabilitation
        after critical illness. Confidence: HIGH.

UNKNOWN (individual): Whether any specific effects apply to you. Severity.
Duration. Impact on work capability.


2.5 EATING AND NUTRITION RECOVERY

After oesophageal surgery and feeding tube removal:

  - Eating may feel different. Swallowing may require more care initially.
  - Smaller, more frequent meals may be needed.
  - Certain foods may be difficult initially.
  - A regular eating schedule may be important for energy management.

Source: General surgical recovery guidance. Confidence: MEDIUM.

UNKNOWN (individual): Current eating capability. Dietary restrictions. Need
for scheduled meal breaks.


2.6 FATIGUE AND ENERGY MANAGEMENT

After major surgery and prolonged hospitalisation:

  - Fatigue is expected and normal.
  - Energy levels typically improve gradually over months, but the timeline
    varies significantly between individuals.
  - "Boom and bust" patterns are common — feeling well one day and exhausted
    the next.
  - Pacing (balancing activity with rest) is the recommended approach.

Source: NHS guidance on post-surgical recovery. Confidence: HIGH.

UNKNOWN (individual): Current energy levels. Stamina across a working day.
Recovery variability pattern.


2.7 THINGS NOBODY CAN KNOW WITHOUT CURRENT MEDICAL ASSESSMENT

The following require assessment by a qualified healthcare professional
(GP, consultant, or Occupational Health physician). No one can determine
these from medical history alone:

  - Whether you currently meet the Equality Act 2010 definition of disability
  - Your current fitness for work (any work)
  - Your fitness for your specific previous role
  - What adjustments would be appropriate
  - How many hours you could sustain
  - Your recovery trajectory over the next 3, 6, or 12 months
  - Your risk of relapse or complication
  - Whether you can safely perform emergency-response duties
  - Whether you require permanent restrictions

These are clinical judgments, not administrative ones. It is appropriate and
responsible to say: "I would prefer that to be assessed by a medical
professional" if such questions arise.


# PART 3 — OCCUPATIONAL HEALTH PREPARATION


3.1 WHAT OCCUPATIONAL HEALTH (OH) DOES

Occupational Health is an independent medical service that advises both
employers and employees about:

  - Whether an employee is fit for work
  - What adjustments or support might help an employee return or stay at work
  - How work might affect an employee's health
  - Whether the workplace presents any risks to the employee's health

OH professionals are usually specialist nurses or doctors with additional
training in occupational medicine.

Source: NHS — Occupational Health services. Confidence: HIGH.


3.2 WHAT OH CAN RECOMMEND

OH can recommend:

  - A phased return to work (gradually building up hours)
  - Reduced hours (temporary or permanent)
  - Altered duties (modifying specific tasks)
  - Additional rest breaks
  - Changes to the working environment
  - Equipment or adaptations
  - A trial period with review
  - That you are not currently fit for work
  - A further review after a specified period

Source: ACAS — Reasonable adjustments at work (acas.org.uk, 2025).


3.3 WHAT OH CANNOT DECIDE

OH does NOT decide:

  - Whether you keep your job
  - Whether adjustments are "reasonable" for the employer
  - Whether to dismiss you
  - Your pay during a phased return
  - Your pension or retirement arrangements

OH provides medical advice. The employer makes the employment decisions.

Source: ACAS guidance. Confidence: HIGH.


3.4 HOW AN OH ASSESSMENT WORKS

  - The employer refers you (with your consent).
  - You attend an appointment (may be face-to-face or by phone/video).
  - The OH professional reviews your medical history and current status.
  - They may ask about your role and what it involves.
  - They produce a report with recommendations.
  - YOU see the report before it goes to your employer. You can ask for
    factual corrections if something is wrong.
  - The report goes to the employer. They consider the recommendations.

Source: NHS and ACAS guidance. Confidence: HIGH.


3.5 WHAT EMPLOYERS NORMALLY ASK OH

The employer's referral form typically asks:

  - Is the employee fit to return to work?
  - If not, when might they be fit?
  - What adjustments are recommended?
  - Is the condition likely to be covered by the Equality Act 2010?
  - What is the long-term prognosis for work capability?
  - Are there any workplace risks to the employee?

Source: Standard OH referral practice. Confidence: HIGH.


3.6 LIKELY QUESTIONS AND EVIDENCE-BASED WAYS TO ANSWER

QUESTION: "How are you feeling now?"
ANSWER APPROACH: Describe what you can do, not what you can't. "My recovery
has been steady. I'm managing daily activities at home and my energy is
gradually improving. I'm not back to where I was before, but I am moving in
the right direction."

QUESTION: "When do you think you might be ready to return?"
ANSWER APPROACH: "I'd prefer to let a medical assessment guide that. I'm keen
to return when it is safe and appropriate, but I don't want to guess at a
timeline that may not be realistic."

QUESTION: "What do you think you could manage?"
ANSWER APPROACH: "I haven't tested myself in a work environment yet, so I
think it would be best to start with a conversation about what might work,
perhaps through a phased approach, and review as we go."

QUESTION: "Do you have any ongoing symptoms?"
ANSWER APPROACH: Be factual. "I still experience fatigue, which is expected
after this type of recovery. I'm learning to manage it. I would want any
return plan to account for that."

QUESTION: "Are you under any treatment restrictions?"
ANSWER APPROACH: If yes, state them. If no, say so. "My treatment is
complete. I am not under any specific restrictions, but I am still building
strength and stamina."

PRINCIPLE: Never guess. Never over-promise. Never under-sell your recovery.
State what you know. Defer what you don't to medical assessment.


# PART 4 — EMPLOYMENT RIGHTS (ENGLAND)


This section explains the legal framework in England. This is informational
only — NOT legal advice. For specific advice about your situation, contact
ACAS, your trade union, or a solicitor.


4.1 EQUALITY ACT 2010 — DISABILITY PROTECTION

Under the Equality Act 2010, you are considered disabled if you have:

  - A physical or mental impairment
  - That has a "substantial" (more than minor or trivial) negative effect
  - On your ability to do normal daily activities
  - That is "long-term" (has lasted or is likely to last 12 months or more)

Your condition began in October 2025 (approximately 8 months ago). Whether you
meet the definition depends on current impact and expected duration — this is
a medical question.

If you ARE covered, your employer has a legal duty to make "reasonable
adjustments" to remove disadvantages you face at work.

If you are NOT currently covered but the condition is expected to last 12
months, you may still be protected under the "long-term" test.

Source: GOV.UK — Definition of disability under the Equality Act 2010
        (gov.uk, 2026). Confidence: HIGH.

FACT: The Equality Act 2010 protects against discrimination because of
disability, including:
  - Direct discrimination
  - Discrimination arising from disability
  - Failure to make reasonable adjustments
  - Harassment
  - Victimisation


4.2 AGE DISCRIMINATION PROTECTION

The Equality Act 2010 also protects against age discrimination.

  - There is NO default retirement age in the UK. It was abolished in 2011.
  - You can work as long as you want to.
  - An employer can only force retirement at a specific age if they can show
    a "good reason" — known as "objective justification" (e.g., the job
    requires certain physical capabilities that diminish with age, or there
    is a legal age limit for the role).
  - School office/clerical work would be very difficult to justify an age
    limit for.

Source: GOV.UK — Working after State Pension age (gov.uk, 2026).
        Confidence: HIGH.


4.3 SICKNESS ABSENCE PROCESS

  - After 7 days of sickness, you need a fit note from a healthcare
    professional (GP, hospital doctor, nurse, occupational therapist,
    pharmacist, or physiotherapist).
  - A fit note may say "not fit for work" or "may be fit for work."
  - If it says "may be fit for work," the employer should discuss changes
    that could enable a return (e.g., different hours, amended duties).
  - If no agreement is reached, you are treated as "not fit for work."
  - After 4 weeks, absence is considered "long-term sick."

Source: GOV.UK — Taking sick leave (gov.uk, 2026). Confidence: HIGH.


4.4 REASONABLE ADJUSTMENTS

If you are disabled under the Equality Act, your employer must make reasonable
adjustments. These can include:

  - Changing working patterns (e.g., reduced hours, flexible start/finish)
  - Changing the workplace (e.g., accessible desk location on ground floor)
  - Providing equipment or support
  - Redistributing duties within the team
  - A phased return to work
  - Allowing time off for medical appointments
  - Adjusting absence triggers or policies

The employer must make adjustments when they know (or should reasonably know)
about your disability. They do not need to make adjustments that are
unreasonable (considering cost, practicality, effectiveness, and health and
safety of others).

The EMPLOYER pays for reasonable adjustments. The Access to Work government
scheme may provide additional funding for equipment or support.

Source: ACAS — Reasonable adjustments at work (acas.org.uk, 2025); GOV.UK —
        Reasonable adjustments for workers with disabilities or health
        conditions (gov.uk, 2026). Confidence: HIGH.


4.5 CAPABILITY PROCESS AND DISMISSAL

An employer CAN dismiss an employee who is long-term sick — BUT only as a
last resort, and only after:

  - Considering whether the employee can return to work (including flexible
    working, part-time, different duties, with training if needed)
  - Consulting with the employee about when they could return and whether
    their health will improve
  - Obtaining medical evidence (usually via Occupational Health)
  - Following a fair procedure (meetings, right to be accompanied, right
    to appeal)

Unfair dismissal claims can be brought to an employment tribunal. You must
have at least 2 years' continuous service (for employment starting after
6 April 2012) to claim ordinary unfair dismissal. BUT claims related to
discrimination (disability, age) have NO qualifying period.

Source: GOV.UK — Taking sick leave (gov.uk, 2026); GOV.UK — Dismissal: your
        rights (gov.uk, 2026). Confidence: HIGH.


4.6 REPRESENTATION RIGHTS

  - If a meeting is formal (capability, disciplinary, grievance), you have
    a statutory right to be accompanied by a trade union representative or
    a work colleague.
  - You do NOT have a statutory right to be accompanied at an informal
    meeting — though you can ask.
  - If you are a member of a trade union, contact them now. They can advise
    and may accompany you.

Source: ACAS — Right to be accompanied. Confidence: HIGH.


4.7 SUMMARY: EMPLOYER CAN ASK / SHOULD NOT ASSUME / EMPLOYEE MAY SAY

EMPLOYER CAN ASK:
  - How is your recovery going? (FACT: they can ask about wellbeing)
  - When might you be able to return to work? (FACT: legitimate question)
  - What support or adjustments might you need? (FACT: they should ask this)
  - Would you consent to an Occupational Health referral? (FACT: they can ask)
  - Do you have a current fit note / medical update? (FACT: they can ask)

EMPLOYER SHOULD NOT ASSUME:
  - That you cannot do your job. (PRACTICE: must be based on medical evidence)
  - That you are ready to return at full capacity. (PRACTICE: must be assessed)
  - That your age means you should retire. (LAW: age discrimination)
  - That a phased return means reduced pay without discussion. (PRACTICE: pay
    during phased return should be agreed)
  - That your condition will not improve. (PRACTICE: must be based on medical
    evidence)

EMPLOYEE MAY SAY:
  - "I'm keen to return, and I'd like medical guidance on the safest way."
  - "I'm not in a position to commit to a timeline today — I'd like to follow
    the Occupational Health process."
  - "I'd like to understand what support options are available before making
    any decisions."
  - "I'm not making retirement decisions at this point."
  - "Could we agree a plan to review this after I've had further medical
    input?"


# PART 5 — RETURN TO WORK SCENARIOS


Based on ACAS and NHS guidance, here are the realistic options for returning
to work after long-term sickness. None assume your capability — they describe
what frameworks exist.


5.1 PHASED RETURN TO WORK

HOW IT WORKS:
  - You gradually build up to your normal hours and duties over an agreed
    period.
  - Example: Week 1-2: 2 hours/day; Week 3-4: 4 hours/day; Week 5-6:
    6 hours/day; then review.
  - The plan is agreed between you and your employer, ideally informed by
    OH advice and/or your fit note.

ADVANTAGES:
  - Tests your stamina without committing to full hours immediately.
  - Allows adjustment of the plan as you go.
  - Reduces risk of relapse or setback.
  - Demonstrates your willingness to return.

RISKS:
  - Pay during non-worked hours depends on agreement/policy — could be full
    pay, company sick pay, or SSP.
  - The plan may need extending — this should be discussed, not treated as
    failure.

TYPICAL APPROVAL: Agreement between employee and employer. Ideally documented
in writing (email or letter).

Source: ACAS — Phased returns (acas.org.uk, 2024). Confidence: HIGH.


5.2 REDUCED HOURS (TEMPORARY OR PERMANENT)

HOW IT WORKS:
  - You work fewer hours than your contracted hours, either temporarily or as
    a permanent change to your contract.
  - If temporary: reviewed at intervals and increased when appropriate.
  - If permanent: a contractual change (get it in writing).

ADVANTAGES:
  - Provides stable, predictable schedule.
  - Allows you to plan around energy levels.

RISKS:
  - Pay is proportional to hours worked (reduced accordingly).
  - Ensure it is clear whether the change is temporary or permanent.

TYPICAL APPROVAL: Agreement with employer. May require OH recommendation.


5.3 ALTERED DUTIES

HOW IT WORKS:
  - Some tasks from your previous role are temporarily or permanently removed,
    reduced, or modified.
  - Examples: reduced standing time; no toilet-supervision duties during
    breaks initially; reduced front-desk hours; fewer telephone-intensive
    periods.

ADVANTAGES:
  - Allows you to return to the workplace while protecting your recovery.
  - Keeps you engaged with your team and routine.

RISKS:
  - May create workload for colleagues — this is the employer's issue to
    manage, not yours.
  - Must be clear what you ARE expected to do and what you are NOT.

TYPICAL APPROVAL: OH recommendation + employer agreement.


5.4 ADDITIONAL REST BREAKS

HOW IT WORKS:
  - You are given more frequent or longer rest breaks than standard.
  - May be for eating, resting, or managing fatigue.

ADVANTAGES:
  - Simple to implement. No cost.

RISKS:
  - Minimal. You should agree when and how long.

TYPICAL APPROVAL: Simple agreement. Document it.


5.5 TRIAL PERIOD

HOW IT WORKS:
  - A defined period (e.g., 4 weeks) during which you try returning under
    agreed conditions.
  - At the end, you and the employer review how it went and decide next steps.

ADVANTAGES:
  - Low pressure — it's explicitly a trial.
  - Gives both sides evidence about what works.

RISKS:
  - If it doesn't work, the employer may conclude return is not possible.
    Mitigate by ensuring the trial conditions are realistic and well-supported.

TYPICAL APPROVAL: Agreement between employee and employer, ideally with OH
input.


5.6 STAGED INCREASES

HOW IT WORKS:
  - A pre-agreed schedule of increasing hours/duties over a longer period
    (e.g., 3 months).
  - Review points are built in.

ADVANTAGES:
  - Provides clarity and predictability.
  - Reduces anxiety about "what comes next."

RISKS:
  - The schedule may need to be adjusted — build in flexibility.


# PART 6 — INTERVIEW QUESTION BANK (25 QUESTIONS)


For each question: what they may be trying to understand, a safe answer
structure, and things NOT to over-answer.


Q1: "How are you feeling now?"
THEY WANT: A general sense of your wellbeing.
SAFE STRUCTURE: Describe recent progress factually. Mention you are improving
without over-claiming.
DON'T OVER-ANSWER: Do not give a detailed medical history. Do not say "I'm
fully recovered" unless confirmed by a doctor and you are certain.


Q2: "How has your recovery been?"
THEY WANT: Understanding of the journey and timeline.
SAFE STRUCTURE: "It's been steady. I spent about 3 months in hospital,
including a period in intensive care, and I've been recovering at home since.
I'm making progress."
DON'T OVER-ANSWER: Do not minimise the severity. It is not in your interest
to make it sound like a minor event.


Q3: "When do you think you might be ready to return to work?"
THEY WANT: A timeline for planning.
SAFE STRUCTURE: "I'd like that to be guided by medical advice. I'm keen to
return when it's safe and appropriate. I'd welcome an Occupational Health
assessment to help determine the right timing and approach."
DON'T OVER-ANSWER: Do not name a date you are not certain of.


Q4: "Do you think you can do your previous role?"
THEY WANT: To understand whether they need to consider adjustments or
alternative roles.
SAFE STRUCTURE: "My aim is to return to my previous role. I think the best
way to answer that is through a gradual return with support, so we can see
what works. I'd like that to be informed by medical advice."
DON'T OVER-ANSWER: Do not say "yes, definitely" or "no, never." You are not
in a position to assess this alone.


Q5: "What adjustments do you think you might need?"
THEY WANT: Practical information for planning.
SAFE STRUCTURE: "Based on my recovery so far, I think a phased return with
reduced hours initially would be sensible. I may need some flexibility around
breaks and energy management. I'd like Occupational Health to advise on what
would be appropriate."
DON'T OVER-ANSWER: Do not list every possible adjustment unprompted. Focus on
what seems most relevant.


Q6: "Have you spoken to your GP or consultant recently?"
THEY WANT: To know if there is up-to-date medical input.
SAFE STRUCTURE: Answer truthfully. If you have a recent fit note or consultant
letter, mention it. If not, say: "I haven't had a recent assessment, which is
why I think an Occupational Health referral would be helpful."
DON'T OVER-ANSWER: Do not guess what your GP would say.


Q7: "Do you have a fit note?"
THEY WANT: Documentation for their records.
SAFE STRUCTURE: Tell them what you have. If you have one that says "may be
fit for work," this is a good basis for discussing adjustments. If it says
"not fit for work," that is the current medical position.
DON'T OVER-ANSWER: The fit note speaks for itself.


Q8: "What if your symptoms return or you have a setback?"
THEY WANT: Reassurance about managing risk.
SAFE STRUCTURE: "I would keep you informed. Recovery isn't always linear, and
I'd want us to have a plan that allows for flexibility. A phased return with
regular reviews would help manage that."
DON'T OVER-ANSWER: Do not promise it won't happen. No one can guarantee that.


Q9: "Do you expect to retire soon?"
THEY WANT: To understand your intentions (and possibly manage staffing
planning).
SAFE STRUCTURE: "I'm not making retirement decisions at the moment. My focus
is on recovery and exploring whether returning to work remains possible. I
hope to continue contributing."
DON'T OVER-ANSWER: Do not volunteer retirement if that is not your intention.
You are not required to discuss retirement in a return-to-work meeting.


Q10: "How do you know you are ready to return?"
THEY WANT: Evidence of readiness.
SAFE STRUCTURE: "I'm feeling stronger and my daily function has improved.
I think the next step is to test that in a supported way — through a phased
return or trial period — rather than trying to predict it from home."
DON'T OVER-ANSWER: Do not claim certainty. Readiness is tested, not declared.


Q11: "Can you manage the physical aspects — standing, walking between areas,
monitoring pupils?"
THEY WANT: To understand functional capacity.
SAFE STRUCTURE: "I'm building my stamina. I think a graduated approach would
be sensible — perhaps starting with office-based duties and gradually
reintroducing the more active parts of the role. I'd like Occupational Health
to advise on this."
DON'T OVER-ANSWER: Do not commit to specific physical capabilities without
testing them.


Q12: "How are you managing fatigue?"

# PART 9 — IF RETIREMENT IS MENTIONED


9.1 UNDERSTANDING THE TERMS

ORDINARY RETIREMENT: You choose to stop working. There is no default retirement
age. You can work as long as you wish. This is entirely your decision.

MEDICAL RETIREMENT / ILL-HEALTH RETIREMENT: This applies where a medical
assessment determines you are permanently unable to do your job (or any job,
depending on the pension scheme rules). This is a medical decision, not an
administrative one. It would typically involve an OH assessment and possibly
input from the pension scheme's medical adviser.

PHASED RETIREMENT: Some employers allow you to reduce your hours gradually
over a period leading to full retirement. This is by agreement — not
compulsory.

CAPABILITY DISMISSAL: The employer terminates your employment on grounds of
capability (health). This is a formal process. It requires medical evidence,
consultation, and a fair procedure. It is a last resort after considering
adjustments and alternatives.

Source: GOV.UK — Working after State Pension age (gov.uk, 2026); ACAS.
Confidence: HIGH.


9.2 NEUTRAL RESPONSES IF RETIREMENT IS RAISED

If the employer raises retirement, you are not required to agree or disagree
in the moment. Consider these responses:

  - "I'm not looking to make retirement decisions today. My current aim is
    to understand my recovery and explore whether returning remains possible."

  - "I'd like to focus on the return-to-work discussion first. Retirement is
    something I'd prefer to consider separately."

  - "I'd like time to consider that. Could we revisit that question at a later
    stage, after I've had further medical input?"

  - "I understand you're asking about my long-term plans. At this point, I'd
    like to explore what's possible with support before considering
    retirement."


9.3 WHEN TO SAY "I'D LIKE TIME TO CONSIDER THAT"

You should use this phrase when:

  - Retirement is raised unexpectedly and you haven't prepared for that
    discussion.
  - You are presented with a proposal, figure, or option you haven't seen
    before.
  - You feel pressure to make a decision on the spot.
  - You want to discuss the matter with family, your GP, your union, or a
    financial adviser.

You do NOT need to give a reason. "I'd like time to consider that" is a
complete sentence.


9.4 DO NOT

  - Do not volunteer that you are considering retirement unless you genuinely
    are and you want to discuss it.
  - Do not agree to retirement to avoid a difficult conversation. You have
    rights and options.
  - Do not assume that because your age has been mentioned, the employer is
    discriminating. They may simply be exploring options. Stay factual.
