## Matter 1 — External Stonework: Damage Assessment & Workmanship Review

### 1.1 Property Overview

4 Bowland View is a new build detached property on a four-plot development at Chipping, built by Hodson Homes Ltd (Glen Hodson, Managing Director). I moved in on 4 July 2025. The property is 2 to 2.5 storeys, traditionally constructed with a cavity wall system: 100mm blockwork inner leaf, 150mm partially insulated cavity, and a 120mm outer leaf of naturally dressed, regularly coursed Carboniferous Millstone Grit (medium-grained sandstone, buff/yellow). The stone specification confirms a compressive strength of 71 MPa and water absorption of 5.4%, consistent with a high-durability masonry product.

There are three identical properties immediately adjacent on the same development, which means any variation in the external stonework appearance is immediately apparent by direct comparison.

### 1.2 Background — How the Damage Occurred

On completion of the build, a cleaning contractor engaged and paid for by Hodson Homes pressure-washed the external stonework to remove mortar drops and construction staining. No chemical agents were used. Following the wash, extensive damage became visible across multiple elevations, including:

- Significant linear grooves and score marks to stone faces
- Chipped arises (edges) to multiple stones
- Open and deficient mortar joints
- Defective embedment of cavity wall vents

A structural inspection was subsequently commissioned by Hodson Homes from Graham Schofield Associates (GSA), Leyland (report ref: GS/ls/2025.171, dated 29 September 2025). GSA's brief was specifically to assess whether the stonework remained structurally sound and capable of performing as a rainscreen — it was not instructed to assess aesthetic impact, workmanship quality, or remediation costs. GSA concluded that the wall construction was structurally acceptable and that the stone would perform adequately as a rainscreen. It noted that the features observed were likely pre-existing in the stone and had been revealed by the pressure wash, and that natural weathering would reduce their visibility over time.

I dispute the implication that this is simply a weathering matter. The pressure wash was carried out by a contractor engaged by the builder; the damage was not present or visible in this form prior to that work. The stone's surface texture and dressed face have been materially altered, and I do not accept that natural weathering constitutes adequate remediation for a new build property.

### 1.3 Failed Remedial Attempt

Following correspondence, Hodson Homes engaged a specialist stone operative (Peter) to carry out a test repair by removing one damaged stone and re-dressing it. During this process:

- I raised specific concern in advance that the cutting method could breach the cavity insulation
- The operative gave me an explicit verbal assurance this was not possible given the tools and dimensions involved
- The insulation was breached regardless — the operative used an approximately 12-inch masonry bit on a hammer drill with no depth stop
- The breach was subsequently rectified with foil tape

The test repair result is visually improved compared to the current state of the affected areas, but I have a fundamental concern about the proposed method at scale. The operative described the stone as "very workable," and the proposed approach involves wire brush re-dressing using an angle grinder or similar. My concern is that this removes material and smooths the surface rather than restoring the original dressed face. The original quarry-dressed texture is produced by chisel, hammer, and scribing tool — it cannot be replicated by grinding. Over time, treated stones dressed in this way are likely to weather differently from undisturbed stones and from the identical properties next door.

I have also observed that the stonework on the front elevation has developed a green tinge that is not present on other elevations. I have not received a satisfactory explanation for this.

### 1.4 Current Position

Hodson Homes has proposed two options:

1. They carry out the re-dressing and selective stone replacement themselves, using Peter as the operative
2. They pay me a cash settlement of **£3,830** and leave the replacement stones for me to arrange the work myself

The £3,830 figure breaks down as:
- 10 days stonework labour @ £190/day = £1,900
- Materials (sand, cement, mixer, access, misc) = £550
- 2 days mortar repair labour @ £190/day = £380
- Inconvenience allowance = £1,000

I have communicated that I am not in a position to accept either option without independent professional advice, and have asked Hodson Homes to pause all further stonework activity pending that assessment.

### 1.5 What I Am Asking Hermolle Associates to Assess

I am seeking an independent professional opinion on two things:

**A — Damage Assessment & Remediation Cost**  
An assessment of the nature and extent of the damage caused by the pressure washing (and subsequent remedial attempts), and an independent view on the realistic cost of restoring the stonework to an acceptable standard — whether that means stone replacement, localised re-dressing, or a combination. I want to understand whether Glen's £3,830 figure is a reasonable reflection of the actual scope and cost, or whether it significantly undersells what proper remediation would require.

**B — Original Workmanship Quality**  
An assessment of the standard of the original stonework installation — specifically whether the quality of laying, coursing, jointing, and finishing is consistent with what would reasonably be expected on a property of this type and specification. I have a broader snag list (available on request) which includes reference to open joints, defective vent embedment, and inconsistencies in the pointing that were identified by the GSA report.

### 2.2 Existing Planning History and Available Survey Data

This is particularly relevant to sequencing and cost. A significant body of technical work was commissioned as part of the 3/2022/0568 application and is held on the public RVBC planning portal. Much of this directly covers the land I own.

**Phase II Geo-Environmental Assessment** (E3P Environmental, July 2022 — ref: 12-424-R1-2)  
A detailed intrusive ground investigation covering the full development parcel, including my land. Findings directly relevant to a new dwelling:
- No made ground or contamination of significance encountered
- Natural drift deposits (sandy silty clayey gravel) confirmed as clean and suitable for re-use
- Allowable bearing pressure of approximately 150–200 kN/m² at 1m depth — adequate for traditional strip or trench fill foundations
- Groundwater encountered as minor seepages at 0.9–2.0m depth in localised areas
- Soakaway drainage not recommended due to clay content in gravel matrix
- One isolated hotspot of Dibenzo(a,h)Anthracene identified at shallow depth in one trial pit — assessed as treatable by hotspot removal during enabling works
- Slope stability flagged as requiring a specific modelled assessment once final development levels are known

This report provides a strong baseline and substantially reduces the scope of any new ground investigation required, though a plot-specific foundation schedule and updated slope stability model would still be needed once a scheme design is established.

**Topographical Survey** — carried out as part of the 3/2022/0568 application and on the public file. However, the land has been heavily disturbed during the build, so a new topo will likely be needed to establish current levels accurately, though the original can serve as a useful baseline.

**Slope Stability Report** (July 2022) — commissioned for the wider parcel and submitted with the planning application. Directly relevant to the sloping nature of my land and any retaining structure design.

**Drainage Strategy** — submitted and approved as part of the application. The approved drainage approach for the wider site is on the planning file and provides context for any new plot drainage design, though a separate strategy would be required for a new dwelling.

**Flood Risk Assessment** (July 2022) — submitted with the application. Chipping Brook runs along the northern boundary of the wider site. The FRA confirmed the development area is not at risk from river flooding, which is relevant context for any new scheme.

**Heritage Statement** — submitted and approved. The wider site context and heritage assessment is on record.

**Ecology surveys** — bat roost survey (2021), ecology report (July 2022), badger, otter, newt, and water vole information all submitted with the application. These are now approaching four years old. Standard practice is that protected species surveys (particularly bats) are valid for 1–3 years depending on the survey type. These will almost certainly need to be refreshed for a new planning application, even if they can be used as background context.

**Tree survey and protection plans** — submitted with the application. As the trees on my land are being removed, the original survey is largely superseded, but the wider tree constraints plan provides useful context regarding boundary trees.

**Highways** — LCC Highways were consulted and the access from Malt Kiln Brow was assessed and agreed as part of the S278 highway works for the original development. This establishes the access point as an approved and engineered junction, which is a significant advantage for any new application.
